⚔ Historic Sites & Stories

The Great Chain Across the Hudson

In 1778, the Continental Army stretched a massive iron chain from West Point to Constitution Island — the most critical defensive engineering of the entire Revolution. The chain, with links weighing 100-150 lbs each, was designed to block British warships from sailing upriver and splitting the colonies. It held for the remainder of the war. The British never breached it.

You can see links from the original chain at Trophy Point, West Point.

West Point Foundry (Cold Spring)

Established 1818 after the War of 1812 exposed America's dependence on foreign arms. The foundry produced some of the nation's first locomotives, ironclad warships, and NYC's water pipes. During the Civil War, 1,400 workers made 2,000 cannons and 3 million shells. The Parrott gun, invented here by Robert Parker Parrott, gave the Union a decisive artillery advantage. President Lincoln visited in 1862.

Visit the ruins: West Point Foundry Preserve, 68 Kemble Ave (free, open dawn-dusk)

Bannerman Castle & Pollepel Island

Francis Bannerman VI, a Scottish immigrant, became America's largest private arms dealer by buying military surplus — including 90% of all captured Spanish-American War weapons. When NYC outlawed storing that much ammo within city limits, he bought Pollepel Island in 1900 and built a Scottish castle as both warehouse and advertisement ("BANNERMAN'S ISLAND ARSENAL" cast into the river-facing wall). Construction ceased at his death in 1918. In 1920, 200+ lbs of shells exploded, destroying the northwest wing. The ruins stand today as one of the Hudson's most haunting landmarks.

Fort Clinton & Fort Montgomery (1777)

Twin Revolutionary War fortifications near Bear Mountain, garrisoned by 600 troops under brothers George and James Clinton. On October 6, 1777, British General Sir Henry Clinton attacked as a diversion from Burgoyne's campaign. The British captured both forts — but the diversion failed. The Americans defeated Burgoyne at Saratoga, the war's turning point.

Fort Montgomery State Historic Site: 690 Route 9W, Fort Montgomery. Free admission. Free guided tours Sat at 11 AM, 1 PM, 3 PM; Sun at 1 PM, 3 PM. ~15 min south of Cold Spring.

📍 Map

The Wappinger People

Before Europeans arrived, the Hudson Highlands were home to the Wappinger, an Eastern Algonquian-speaking people of the Lenape nation. Cold Spring was a "decent-sized village" with several satellite communities. Denning's Point in Beacon was a major intertribal trading place. The Wappinger lived semi-sedentarily, moving between seasonal sites — women cultivated corn while hunting, fishing, and foraging supplemented the diet. After the 1685 Rombout Royal Patent granted 85,000 acres to colonists, the Wappinger were gradually displaced, joining the Lenape, Mohicans, or Iroquois by the Revolutionary War era.

West Point Military Academy

America's oldest continuously occupied military post (since 1778). Washington called it "the most important post in America." The fortress controlled the narrowest point of the Hudson. Benedict Arnold's plot to surrender it to the British in 1780 was one of the most dramatic espionage events of the war. Today it trains ~4,400 cadets. The 2026 250th Anniversary tour is a special offering.

Visitor Center: Open daily 9 AM - 4:45 PM. REAL ID required.

Washington's Headquarters (Newburgh)

The Hasbrouck House, where Washington headquartered from April 1782 to August 1783 — his longest stay at any HQ during the war. Here he rejected the offer of kingship (the Nicola Letter) and issued the Proclamation of Peace.

Address: 84 Liberty St, Newburgh • Sunday hours: 1-5 PM • Cost: $7 adult

The Hasbrouck House itself is closed for restoration (indefinite). The museum building is open.
